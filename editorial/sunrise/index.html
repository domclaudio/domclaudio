Content Strategy Sample For the JPL Copywriter/Content Strategist role · APR Consulting

Watching the Sun,
in time to act.

A second editing exercise on a NASA mission page. Same discipline as the NISAR sample, applied to a different editorial problem: a science page that buries its human stakes.

SOURCE: Mission overview text, SunRISE page — jpl.nasa.gov/missions/sun-radio-interferometer-space-experiment REFERENCE: Artemis II radiation forecasting, University of Michigan Engineering — news.umich.edu
Original ~360 words · 5 paragraphs

The Sun Radio Interferometer Space Experiment, or SunRISE, is an array of six toaster-size CubeSats that will work together to study solar activity. The mission will observe low radio frequency emissions so scientists can understand better how the Sun is able to generate intense space weather storms – known as solar particle storms – that can be hazardous to spacecraft and astronauts. This research will help scientists forecast space weather, improve our understanding of how our Sun works, and may apply to studies of other stars – particularly those with planets.

The SunRISE spacecraft will orbit within 6 miles (10 kilometers) of one another well above Earth's atmosphere to act like a giant single-aperture radio telescope. The SunRISE spacecraft have to be at a high altitude because an upper layer of Earth's atmosphere, known as the ionosphere, reflects low radio frequencies, preventing the radio emission associated with solar particle storms to be studied from the ground. The constellation of small spacecraft uses a method known as interferometry, in which many smaller radio telescopes can be combined to mimic a single, much larger observatory with a very high resolving power.

The mission will create detailed 3D maps of where energetic radio emissions occur in the Sun's magnetic atmosphere, which signify the locations of extremely powerful bursts of radiation. By observing these events, SunRISE will help us to better understand what triggers them, how the particles are accelerated, and the ways in which particle storms evolve.

As a NASA Mission of Opportunity, SunRISE will launch in summer 2026 as a rideshare aboard a United Launch Alliance Vulcan Centaur rocket, sponsored by the United States Space Force's Space Systems Command.

SunRISE is led by professor Susan Lepri at the University of Michigan in Ann Arbor and managed by NASA's Jet Propulsion Laboratory, a division of Caltech in Pasadena, California.

Revised ~250 words · 4 paragraphs

Solar storms can fry a satellite and irradiate astronauts, exactly the threat NASA tracked in real time during Artemis 2's ten-day flight around the Moon this April.

The crew came home safe, but the radiation watch was active throughout, monitored by forecasting models built at the University of Michigan. SunRISE is led by a scientist at the same university — Susan Lepri at the University of Michigan — and her mission's purpose is to see these storms coming earlier and more clearly than we can today, so future astronauts have more warning.

Six small spacecraft, each about the size of a toaster, will launch in summer 2026 and fly in a tight formation high above Earth's atmosphere. Spaced just six miles apart, they'll work together as a single radio telescope: the way a stadium full of microphones can pick up a single voice from any seat in the room. They have to fly that high because Earth's atmosphere blocks the kind of low-frequency radio signals SunRISE is built to detect — signals emitted by the Sun's most violent outbursts: bursts of energetic particles that can damage spacecraft, threaten astronauts in orbit, and disrupt communications and power grids on the ground. Scientists know these storms happen, but how they begin to form, and under what circumstances, still remains a mystery.

SunRISE will build the first 3D maps of where these bursts originate in the Sun's magnetic atmosphere, and trace the magnetic field lines that connect the Sun's interior to the rest of the solar system. The data will sharpen space weather forecasts, improve our basic understanding of how stars work, and could eventually help us study other stars with planets of their own. The mission is managed by NASA's Jet Propulsion Laboratory in Pasadena.

Editorial Decisions
1
Lead with stakes, not subject.
The original opens with the mission's full bureaucratic name and a structural description. The actual stakes — solar storms hazardous to astronauts and spacecraft — arrive in the third clause of the second sentence, behind two layers of subordinate construction. The revision opens with the threat itself, then anchors it to a recent named event: Artemis 2's ten-day flight around the Moon this April. A reader who didn't know SunRISE existed now has a reason to care before they meet the technology.
2
Surface the imagery already there.
The original has two image-rich phrases — six toaster-size CubeSats and orbit within 6 miles of one another — both buried inside subordinate clauses. The revision pulls each into its own sentence so it can land. The fix isn't writing new imagery; it's giving the source's own concrete details room to breathe.
3
Translate the technique with an analogy.
"Many smaller radio telescopes can be combined to mimic a single, much larger observatory" is technically accurate but still abstract. A reader who didn't know the term interferometry doesn't really get the technique after reading it. Replaced with: the way a stadium full of microphones can pick up a single voice from any seat in the room. The reader doesn't need to learn the word to understand what's happening.
4
Cut the parenthetical definitions.
"Known as solar particle storms" and "known as the ionosphere" interrupt the rhythm and slightly condescend. The revision either weaves the meaning into surrounding prose (the storms become "bursts of energetic particles that can damage spacecraft, threaten astronauts...") or trusts the reader (the ionosphere becomes simply "Earth's atmosphere"). Mid-sentence vocabulary lessons are almost always a sign the writer is hedging.
5
End on stakes, not logistics.
The original's penultimate paragraph is procurement detail: rideshare aboard a Vulcan Centaur rocket sponsored by the Space Force's Space Systems Command. Bureaucratic trivia after several genuinely interesting paragraphs about science. The revision cuts it entirely. The piece deserves to land on what the science can do; mission management credit (Lepri, JPL) belongs at the end as institutional citation, not a launch-vehicle catalog.
